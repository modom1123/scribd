Case study — handling ambiguity

Talk to ten people.

Two moments at Leadventure, months apart, that show the same instinct: build the structure the ambiguity is missing, then defend it with better reasoning than volume.

Part I — Day one
01 — The brief that wasn't one

Go find out what's actually going on, and tell us what to do about it.

My first day at Leadventure, I was told to talk to ten people and come back with an agenda. Not a project, not a problem to solve — an agenda. It was on me to figure out what that even meant.

02 — What ten conversations built

No template for either one, so the structure came from what kept repeating.

Nobody handed me a format for "agenda." So I built one out of what those ten conversations actually gave me: the same handful of problems and priorities surfacing independently, across people who didn't necessarily agree on much else.

That pattern became a 90-day action plan — and the first product roadmap digital marketing ever had, because until then, digital marketing wasn't treated as a product at Leadventure at all. Not because I arrived with a plan — because I listened for what ten unrelated people kept saying without being asked to agree.

Part II — The trade-off
Months later — after the Tealium contract was signed, early in the internal automation build
03 — The trade-off

Sales wanted $1,200 to redirect the team. The scores did the arguing.

Sales wanted to shift build resources toward a $1,200 national ad campaign — pitched as a foot in the door with an OEM client we'd been trying to land.

The first move wasn't a debate, it was a WSJF pass — weighing the ask against the roadmap on cost of delay versus job size, so both priorities landed on the same scale.

That gap is what sent me looking for why it was so lopsided, not just accepting the ask at face value. The math backed it up: $1,200 doesn't move a national campaign, and pulling engineering off the automation build to stand up the ad tech would cost more than the deal itself was worth — before it even had a chance to close.

I took that to the sales manager first. We didn't land on the same page, so we each took our case to our own leadership. Cases were made on both sides. Mine won on the math.

04 — Why it held

Being right isn't what makes a trade-off stick.

The easy version of this story is "I was right." The real version is that the call held because the reasoning was visible the whole way through — not "this isn't a priority," but here's what the campaign costs, here's what the build costs to interrupt, here's what each one actually returns.

When the reasoning is visible, disagreeing with the call means disagreeing with the arithmetic. That's a very different conversation than disagreeing with a gut decision — and it's the difference between a trade-off that holds and one that gets relitigated every quarter.
